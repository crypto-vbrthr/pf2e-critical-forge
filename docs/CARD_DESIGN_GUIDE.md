# Critical Forge Card Design Guide

## Introduction

This guide describes the design principles used throughout the Critical Forge ecosystem.

It is intended for anyone creating card packs for Critical Forge, whether as part of the official project or as a third-party extension.

Following these guidelines helps ensure that cards remain consistent, balanced and enjoyable to use across different modules.

---

# Core Philosophy

A Critical Forge card should do more than apply a modifier.

Every card should represent a memorable moment during combat.

Good cards create stories.

Instead of:

> Target takes -1 to attacks.

Prefer:

> The blow twists the target's shoulder, making precise strikes difficult until it recovers.

Mechanics exist to support narrative, not replace it.

---

# Design Goals

Cards should:

- create memorable moments
- be mechanically meaningful
- resolve quickly
- avoid slowing combat
- remain compatible with PF2e rules
- encourage tactical decisions

Cards should not:

- permanently punish characters
- remove player agency for extended periods
- destroy equipment without player consent
- require excessive bookkeeping

---

# Card Identity

Each card should have a clear identity.

Avoid creating multiple cards that differ only by numbers.

Instead of:

- Clumsy 1
- Clumsy 2
- Clumsy 3

Create cards with different tactical situations.

---

# Mechanical Variety

Use a healthy mixture of:

- conditions
- positioning
- movement
- reactions
- temporary modifiers
- resistances
- weaknesses
- persistent damage
- action economy

Avoid repeating the same mechanical pattern.

---

# Duration

Most cards should last:

- until the start of the next turn
- until the end of the next turn
- 1 round

Long durations should be uncommon.

Permanent effects should be avoided.

---

# Complexity

A player should understand a card within a few seconds.

Prefer:

- one strong effect

over

- many small effects

---

# Narrative

Cards should explain *why* the mechanical effect happens.

The title, description and mechanics should reinforce each other.

---

# Card Pack Identity

Every card pack should have a clearly defined purpose.

Examples:

Arsenal
- physical consequences
- injuries
- weapon-specific effects

Martial Consequences
- battlefield flow
- positioning
- momentum
- tactical opportunities

Avoid overlapping identities.

---

# Balancing

A powerful effect is acceptable.

An annoying effect is not.

Cards should create exciting situations rather than frustration.

---

# Testing

Every new pack should be reviewed for:

- duplicate mechanics
- duplicate themes
- rules compatibility
- readability
- narrative consistency
- gameplay impact

---

# Final Rule

If a card creates an interesting story that players remember after the session, it is probably a good Critical Forge card.
